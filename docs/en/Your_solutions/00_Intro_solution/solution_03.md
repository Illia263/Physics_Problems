### Problem 3: Proportionality in Universal Gravitation

**Given:**
The original gravitational force equation is:

$$F = G\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}$$

We are told three things happen to create our "new" situation:

1. The distance $r$ is doubled.
2. Mass $m_1$ is halved.
3. Mass $m_2$ is halved.

---

#### Step 1: Define the new variables

Let's create new variables (we will call them $r'$, $m_1'$, and $m_2'$) to represent the changes.

- New distance: $r' = 2r$
- New mass 1: $m_1' = \frac{1}{2}m_1$
- New mass 2: $m_2' = \frac{1}{2}m_2$

#### Step 2: Substitute the new variables into the original formula

Now, we write the equation for the new force ($F_{new}$) using our new variables.

$$F_{new} = G\frac{m_1'm_2'}{(r')^2}$$

Substitute the expressions from Step 1 into this equation:

$$F_{new} = G\frac{(\frac{1}{2}m_1)(\frac{1}{2}m_2)}{(2r)^2}$$

#### Step 3: Simplify the numerator and the denominator

Now, we do the math for the top and bottom parts of the fraction separately.

- **Numerator:** Multiply the masses. $\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$. So, the top becomes $\frac{1}{4}m_1m_2$.
- **Denominator:** Square the entire distance term. $(2r)^2 = 2^2 \cdot r^2 = 4r^2$.

Plug these back into the equation:

$$F_{new} = G\frac{\frac{1}{4}m_1m_2}{4r^2}$$

#### Step 4: Extract the changing factor

To find out how much the force changed, we need to pull all the numbers to the front of the equation, separating them from the original variables ($G$, $m_1$, $m_2$, and $r$).

Divide the fraction $\frac{1}{4}$ by $4$:

$$\frac{\frac{1}{4}}{4} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{16}$$

Bring the $\frac{1}{16}$ to the front:

$$F_{new} = \frac{1}{16} \cdot \left(G\frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}\right)$$

#### Step 5: Compare to the original force

Notice that the part in the parentheses is exactly our original force, $F$.

$$F_{new} = \frac{1}{16}F$$

---

### Final Answer

The force $F$ changes by a factor of **$\frac{1}{16}$**. This means the new gravitational force is 16 times weaker than the original force.
