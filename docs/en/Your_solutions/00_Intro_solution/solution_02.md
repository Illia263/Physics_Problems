### Problem 2: Systems of Equations

Here is the step-by-step solution. We will use the **Substitution Method**, which is very straightforward to explain because you isolate one variable and plug it into the other equation.

**Given System of Equations:**

1. $$2x + 3y = 12$$

2. $$x - y = 1$$

---

#### Step 1: Isolate one variable

It is usually easiest to isolate a variable that doesn't have a number (coefficient) attached to it. Let's look at the second equation and solve for $x$.

$$x - y = 1$$

Add $y$ to both sides to get $x$ by itself:

$$x = y + 1$$

#### Step 2: Substitute into the other equation

Now that we know $x$ is equal to $y + 1$, we can replace the $x$ in the **first equation** with $(y + 1)$.

Original first equation:

$$2x + 3y = 12$$

Substitute $(y + 1)$ for $x$:

$$2(y + 1) + 3y = 12$$

#### Step 3: Solve for $y$

Now we have an equation with only one variable, which we can solve step-by-step.

First, distribute the $2$ into the parentheses:

$$2y + 2 + 3y = 12$$

Combine the like terms ($2y$ and $3y$):

$$5y + 2 = 12$$

Subtract $2$ from both sides:

$$5y = 10$$

Divide by $5$:

$$y = 2$$

#### Step 4: Solve for $x$

Now that we have the value for $y$, we can plug it back into our rearranged equation from Step 1 to find $x$.

$$x = y + 1$$

$$x = 2 + 1$$

$$x = 3$$

#### Step 5: Verify the answer (Optional but highly recommended)

Teachers always appreciate it when you prove your answer is correct. You do this by plugging both values back into the original equations to see if they hold true.

- **Check Equation 1:** $2(3) + 3(2) = 6 + 6 = 12$ (Correct!)
- **Check Equation 2:** $3 - 2 = 1$ (Correct!)

---

### Final Answer

The values that satisfy both equations are:

$$x = 3$$

$$y = 2$$

Geometrically, if you were to graph these two linear equations, the point $(3, 2)$ is exactly where the two lines cross.
