
### Problem 1: Gravitational Dependence

**Part 1: Period on the Moon**

**Given:**
* Period on Earth: $T_E = 4 \text{ s}$
* Gravity on Moon: $g_M = \frac{1}{6}g_E$

**Goal:** Find the period on the Moon ($T_M$).

**Step 1: Use the pendulum period formula**
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

**Step 2: Set up the formula for the Moon**
Substitute $g_M$ into the denominator:
$$T_M = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{\frac{1}{6}g_E}}$$

**Step 3: Simplify and relate to Earth's period**
When dividing by a fraction inside the square root, you multiply by its reciprocal. Bring the $6$ to the top:
$$T_M = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{6L}{g_E}}$$
Factor the $\sqrt{6}$ out to the front:
$$T_M = \sqrt{6} \cdot \left(2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g_E}}\right)$$
Notice that the term in parentheses is exactly the formula for the period on Earth ($T_E$).
$$T_M = \sqrt{6} \cdot T_E$$

**Step 4: Calculate the final value**
$$T_M = \sqrt{6} \cdot 4$$
$$T_M \approx 2.449 \cdot 4$$
$$T_M \approx 9.80 \text{ s}$$

---

**Part 2: Length for a 1-second period on Earth**

**Given:**
* Target Period: $T = 1 \text{ s}$
* Earth's gravity: $g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$

**Goal:** Find the required length ($L$).

**Step 1: Rearrange the period formula for $L$**
Divide by $2\pi$:
$$\frac{T}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
Square both sides:
$$\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2} = \frac{L}{g}$$
Multiply by $g$:
$$L = \frac{g T^2}{4\pi^2}$$

**Step 2: Substitute the values and calculate**
$$L = \frac{9.8 \cdot (1)^2}{4\pi^2}$$
$$L \approx \frac{9.8}{39.478}$$
$$L \approx 0.248 \text{ m}$$ 

---

**Final Answer:**
* **Period on the Moon:** $\approx 9.80 \text{ seconds}$
* **Required pendulum length:** $\approx 0.248 \text{ meters}$ (or 24.8 cm)
